Sphero Partners

Location Client Duration
Boulder, CO Sphero, Inc. 300+ hours

During the summer of 2014, I designed and built the Sphero Partners portal and store to help Sphero expand their reach with resellers and education partners. Since its launch a month ago, the application has processed over $12,000 in sales and over 70 partner applications.

Visit the site

Design challenge

Sphero works with a host of distributors and resellers to get its devices in front of as many faces and on as many shelves as possible. Since Sphero is still a young and growing company, it lacks the employees to closely manage partnerships with many smaller distributors and resellers. When I started my internship at the beginning of the summer, Sphero was passing up many opportunities to work with partners due to the logistical nightmare of bringing on a new partner. My task was to streamline the application and review processes and to develop an automated system for handling bulk orders of Sphero's products.

Process

Inputs

For this project, I spent quite a bit of time analyzing the order flow around McMaster-Carr's impressively organized online catalog and Amazon's frictionless 1-Click process. My goal from the start of the project was to meld the two disparate use-cases into an output that felt both industrial and human-centered.

From the very start, you feel like a pro using the McMaster shop. You don't just create an order, you build it, even if you're only buying a couple screws.

Amazon's 1-Click button is dangerously simple. You don't have to think about any details around your order, only that you want the product you're looking at and you want it now.

Prototypes

I spent about two weeks working full time on prototypes of the interface. In this time period, I explored many alternate options for each interaction before honing in on the options that I felt would be the most practical and efficient. During this period, I also spent time getting acquianted with Sphero's bulk order process for existing partners and pinpointing which parts could be automated and which would require human intervention.

I initially envisioned a dashboard for tracking every step of an order. I later realized the use case would be a lot simpler and that most customers would only manage one or two orders at a time.

This mockup for editing an order proved to be more complicated than necessary, but many elements are present in the final product.

We eventually chose to simplify the payment options to only debit and credit cards in the interest of scability, but I spent some time exploring how a customer might keep track of multiple forms of payments on multiple orders.
